## ✅ **Production Incident – 504 Gateway Timeout**

**Answer:**

### **1️⃣ How I Would Triage the Issue**

Since production is down and users are impacted, I would immediately treat this as a **P1 incident**.

My first step would be to:

*   **Acknowledge the incident**
*   Check monitoring dashboards (CloudWatch, Prometheus/Grafana) to understand:
    *   When the issue started
    *   Whether all users or specific services are impacted
    *   Any correlation with recent deployments or infrastructure changes

The goal of triage is to **quickly identify the scope and narrow down whether this is an ALB, Kubernetes, application, or deployment-related issue**.

***

### **2️⃣ What I Would Check First and Why**

#### ✅ **Step 1: ALB & Target Group Health**

Because users are seeing **504 Gateway Timeout**, my first check would be the **Application Load Balancer**:

*   ALB target group health status
*   Are targets marked **unhealthy**?
*   ALB metrics:
    *   `HTTPCode_Target_5XX`
    *   `TargetResponseTime`

**Reason:**  
A 504 error usually means the ALB is **not getting a response from backend services in time**, so validating whether traffic is reaching healthy targets is critical.

***

#### ✅ **Step 2: Kubernetes Pods and Services**

Next, I’d switch to Kubernetes:

*   `kubectl get pods` to check pod status
*   Look for:
    *   `CrashLoopBackOff`
    *   Frequent restarts
    *   Pods not in `Ready` state
*   Verify services and endpoints:
    *   Ensure endpoints are correctly registered

**Reason:**  
Even if ALB is working, traffic will fail if pods are unhealthy or not exposed through services.

***

#### ✅ **Step 3: Application Logs**

Then I would inspect application logs:

*   Using `kubectl logs` or centralized logging (ELK / CloudWatch)
*   Look for:
    *   Timeout errors
    *   Database or downstream service failures
    *   Memory or thread exhaustion

**Reason:**  
504s often occur due to **slow application responses or dependency failures**, not just infra issues.

***

#### ✅ **Step 4: CI/CD – Recent Deployments**

I would check Jenkins or GitHub Actions:

*   Confirm if a deployment happened recently
*   Review:
    *   Image version changes
    *   Config or environment variable updates
*   If the issue correlates with deployment, I would **rollback to the last stable version**

**Reason:**  
Many production outages are caused by **faulty deployments**, so isolating this early saves time.

***

#### ✅ **Step 5: Resource & Scaling Checks**

Finally:

*   Check CPU/memory for pods and nodes
*   Verify HPA behavior
*   Ensure nodes are not under pressure

**Reason:**  
Resource starvation can cause applications to respond slowly or stop responding completely.

***

### **3️⃣ How I Would Communicate with Stakeholders**

Communication is continuous and transparent:

*   **Initial update:**  
    “We are aware of the production issue and actively investigating.”

*   **During investigation:**
    *   Provide periodic updates
    *   Share impact scope
    *   Avoid giving speculative ETAs

*   **After resolution:**
    *   Communicate resolution
    *   Share Root Cause Analysis (RCA)
    *   Outline preventive actions

This ensures **trust, clarity, and confidence** with stakeholders.
